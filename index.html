Executive Overview
What this project is about
Pitch‑ready summary

Traditional rocket simulations assume a “nice” atmosphere and then push a design through it. In reality, wind shear and density gradients change with altitude and are rarely measured directly, so predictions drift from what rockets actually do.

This project uses physics‑informed neural networks to work backwards from flight data—altitude, acceleration, attitude, and motor data—to reconstruct the atmosphere itself. Instead of treating the atmosphere as a fixed input, it becomes an unknown state inferred by the model. The result is tighter apogee predictions and more informed design and launch decisions under real‑world uncertainty.

Talk Track
How to present it
In 4 beats
  1. Problem – Simulators assume simple atmospheres; real flights see messy winds and density, so predictions are off.
  2. Idea – Treat each rocket flight as an atmospheric experiment and use a physics‑informed neural network to infer what the atmosphere had to be.
  3. Method – Feed in telemetry and rocket parameters, enforce Newton’s laws and drag physics inside the loss function, and solve for altitude‑dependent wind/density that make the trajectory consistent.
  4. Result – Reconstructed atmospheric profiles and smaller apogee prediction error than physics‑only baselines.
